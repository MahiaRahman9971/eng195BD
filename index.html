What is the American Dream?



The American Dream symbolizes the belief that any child,
regardless of their background, can achieve success and upward mobility through hard work and determination.

It represents a vision of freedom and opportunity, where personal and economic fulfillment are attainable goals for all.

Over time, the American dream has slowly been fading.

However, the chances of children achieving upward mobility isn't the same everywhere. It varies heavily across the United States, often at a granular level.

So, why does opportunity vary so much across the United States?
Here are the five strongest correlates of upward mobility:


Segregation

Greater racial and income segregation is associated with less mobility. Concentrated poverty (e.g. public housing) is associated with the lowest levels of mobility.


Income Inequality

Areas with more income inequality within a given generation tend to have less economic mobility across generations. In particular, places with smaller middle class have much less mobility.


School Quality

High expenditure, smaller classes, and higher test scores are correlated with more mobility.


Family Structure

Areas with more single parents have much lower mobility.


Social Capital

It is the extent to which low and high income people are interacting with each other. Communities that very connected across lines may have higher social capital for lower income folks.

The five strongest correlates often present themselves in varied ways depending on race, gender, and parental income.

Now we know opportunity varies significantly based on geographical location, race, gender, income, family structures, and social connections. These attributes cause differing outcomes for children in terms of upward mobility.

So, how do we help children growing up in low-opportunity areas?

The Moving to Opportunity Experiment provides some insight.

What is the Moving to Opportunity Experiment?


The Moving to Opportunity Experiment was implemented by the federal government from 1994-1998 at 5 sites: Baltimore, Boston, Chicago, LA, New York. 4,600 families living in high-poverty public housing projects were randomly assigned to one of three groups:


1. Experimental

Offered housing vouchers restricted to low-poverty (<10%) census tracts. Perhaps growing up in richer neighborhoods will provide better outcomes.


2. Section 8

Offered conventional housing vouchers with no area restrictions. Families provided rental assistance of $1000 a month.


3. Control

Not offered a voucher and remained in public housing.



Central question: Do neighborhoods matter in determining economic opportunity?

Stories from Moving to Opportunity - The Palacios


Source

The Palacio family initially lived in the Linden Houses, a massive public housing structure near the Brownsville border that's home to thousands of families. They made the move in 1983, when Audra was 6. "I remember when we moved into the Nehemiahs. We were so excited. We had rooms. We had space. We had a backyard!" When she moved her family to the Nehemiah houses (a higher opportunity area), they didn't just get a beautiful backyard and spacious living room. They got a place to play safely with their friends; a quiet area to do their homework; a good night's sleep.

Conclusion

Transforming the trajectories of children from low-opportunity areas by relocating them to high-opportunity areas creates many long-term advantages. Providing these children with enhanced resources and a superior quality of life not only enriches their immediate circumstances but also opens doors to a future with greater possibilities and the potential for a more fulfilling life.
